## Not Just a Bigger House

It's a fair assumption to make: painting is painting. You hire someone with rollers and brushes, they cover the surface, job done. But if you manage or own a commercial property in Fort Collins or Northern Colorado, that assumption can cost you — in downtime, failed coatings, liability exposure, and rework.

Commercial exterior painting is a fundamentally different discipline. The surfaces are different, the coatings are different, the equipment is different, and the project management requirements are in a different category entirely. A contractor who does excellent work on residential homes isn't automatically equipped to handle a commercial job — and the gap between the two shows up fast.

Here's what actually separates commercial and residential exterior painting, and why it matters when you're choosing a contractor.

---

## Crew Size and Scheduling Pressure

Residential painting runs on a crew of two to four people working over a few days. Commercial jobs don't have that luxury.

Commercial properties have operational windows. A retail center can't have scaffolding blocking the entrance on a Saturday. An HOA common building may need to stay functional throughout the work. A multi-unit property has tenants with schedules to respect.

Commercial contractors staff and schedule accordingly — larger crews, phased approaches, and project timelines built around your business operations, not just weather windows. That kind of coordination requires experience. A residential crew dropped into a commercial job typically underestimates the logistical complexity before the first coat goes on.

---

## Equipment Is Not Interchangeable

Residential painters primarily use brush, roller, and small sprayers. Commercial work at scale demands industrial airless sprayers, boom lifts, scaffolding systems, and sometimes aerial equipment — all operated by crews trained to use them safely and efficiently.

Beyond the equipment itself, Northern Colorado's altitude adds a layer of complexity. UV radiation runs 10–15% higher than at lower elevations, and coatings behave differently when applied at elevation and in variable temperature conditions. Commercial crews working in Fort Collins, Windsor, and the surrounding region need to account for these factors in their application approach — not just their paint selection.

---

## Coatings Specs: Commercial Grade vs. Residential Grade

Walk into any paint supplier and you'll find a divide between residential and commercial product lines. The difference isn't just marketing — it's formulation.

Commercial coatings are engineered for higher-traffic surfaces, greater film builds, resistance to impact and abrasion, and longer service intervals. Elastomeric coatings, masonry sealers, and two-part epoxy systems used on commercial substrates don't have a residential equivalent.

That means products rated for the freeze-thaw stress this region sees, the UV load at altitude, and the surface types common in commercial construction.

---

## Surface Types Require Different Preparation

Residential exteriors are mostly wood siding, fiber cement, and stucco. Commercial exteriors throw in concrete tilt-up, CMU block, EIFS, metal panel, and brick — often on the same building.

Each surface has a different prep protocol, primer requirement, and coating compatibility. Applying a residential-spec product to a concrete masonry unit won't hold. Skipping proper surface profiling on EIFS leads to adhesion failure. These aren't edge cases — they're the job.

A contractor without commercial surface experience is making it up as they go. That shows up in the finished product, usually within the first year.

---
