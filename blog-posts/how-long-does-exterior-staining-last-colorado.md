If you've had a fence or deck stained and you're wondering when you'll need to do it again — you're asking the right question. The honest answer is: it depends. But in Colorado specifically, stain breaks down faster than most product labels suggest. Here's a realistic breakdown by product type, surface, and the factors that accelerate wear in Northern Colorado.

## The Short Answer: Stain Lifespan by Product Type

Stain isn't one-size-fits-all. The formulation you choose has a major impact on how long it holds up.

**Solid-body stain** sits closest to paint. It forms a film over the wood rather than soaking in, which means better UV and moisture protection — but it can peel if the surface wasn't prepped properly. Expect **4–6 years** on a well-prepped surface.

**Semi-transparent stain** penetrates the wood and lets the grain show through. It's a popular choice for fences and decks because it looks natural. Lifespan ranges from **2–4 years**, with Colorado UV pushing that closer to the lower end on south- and west-facing surfaces.

**Transparent or clear stain** offers minimal pigment and therefore minimal UV protection. On a shaded surface in mild climates, you might get 2 years. In Colorado's high-altitude sun? **1–2 years** is realistic, sometimes less.

## How Colorado's Climate Cuts Stain Life Short

Northern Colorado sits at roughly 5,000 feet. That altitude means UV radiation runs **10–15% higher** than at sea level — and UV is the primary enemy of exterior stain. The pigment that gives stain its color and UV-blocking ability degrades faster here than the product manufacturers tested for (most testing happens at sea level).

Add in 28+ freeze-thaw cycles per winter, and you've got a recipe for accelerated wear. Water works into wood grain, freezes, expands, and breaks the stain's bond from the inside out. By spring, surfaces that looked fine in October can show cracking, graying, or bare spots.

If your stained surfaces face south or west — toward direct afternoon sun — subtract a year from any timeline above. [Northern Colorado's exterior staining](https://timnathpainting.com/exterior-staining/) requires a different approach than what you'd find in a milder climate, and any contractor pricing your job should factor that in.

## Surface Type Matters Too

**Wood fences** take a beating. They're typically thinner boards with end grain exposed at the top, which drinks in moisture. A cedar fence stained with a quality semi-transparent product in this climate realistically needs a refresh every **2–3 years**.

**Decks** vary widely by wood species and use. Pressure-treated pine holds stain differently than cedar or redwood. High-traffic decks near sliding doors wear faster than low-traffic side sections. Plan for **2–4 years** depending on the product and exposure.

**T1-11 siding** and rough-sawn wood on sheds or outbuildings can hold solid stain reasonably well — often **4–5 years** — but the texture means surface prep before any reapplication is critical. Stain over chalky or failing product won't bond.

## Signs Your Stain Is Ready for Refreshing

You don't always need to wait for a scheduled timeline. Watch for these signs:

- **Graying or silvering** — the wood is losing its finish and UV protection
- **Water no longer beads** on the surface — the water repellent is gone
- **Cracking or flaking** — especially at exposed edges and end grain
- **Faded color** with visible uneven patches from sun exposure
- **Bare spots** where stain has worn through entirely

If you catch it early — before the wood starts to gray significantly — a light clean and recoat is relatively straightforward. Wait too long and you're looking at more aggressive prep work, or in some cases full stripping before a new product will adhere properly.

## What to Expect From a Quality Stain System in Colorado

The goal isn't just picking the right product — it's applying it correctly to a properly prepared surface. That means cleaning off mold, mildew, and old failing stain, allowing the wood to dry fully, and applying the right product in the right weather window (not too hot, not too cold, no rain in the forecast).
