## Why HOA Exterior Painting Is a Different Animal

Painting a single home is straightforward. Painting 40, 80, or 200 units across an HOA community is an entirely different project — and most painting contractors aren't equipped for it.

HOA exterior painting projects involve coordinated scheduling across multiple buildings, approval from a board or architectural committee, strict insurance requirements, and the challenge of keeping dozens of homeowners informed throughout the process. When something goes sideways — wrong color approved, unit skipped, contractor drops coverage mid-job — the fallout lands on the board.

If your HOA in Timnath, Windsor, or anywhere in Northern Colorado is planning an exterior repaint, here's what you need to know before you sign anything.

---

## The Bidding Process: What to Look For

Most HOAs require competitive bids before approving a painting contract. That's smart policy — but the lowest bid isn't always the right one.

When evaluating bids for [HOA painting projects](/hoa-painting/), look beyond the bottom-line number:

- **Scope of work clarity** — Does the bid specify surface prep, number of coats, and product brands? Vague bids lead to disputes.
- **Insurance minimums** — Most HOA management companies require $1M–$2M in general liability coverage at minimum. We carry **$2M in liability coverage**, which meets or exceeds most community requirements.
- **License verification** — Colorado requires residential contractors to be licensed. Ask for the license number and verify it.
- **References from similar-scale projects** — A contractor who's painted single-family homes all year may not have the crew size or logistics experience for 60-unit complex.

Don't let a tight budget push you toward a contractor who can't document their credentials. The risk to the community — both financially and aesthetically — isn't worth it.

## Phased Scheduling: How Large Projects Stay on Track

HOA communities rarely get painted all at once. Most large-scale exterior projects are broken into phases — by building cluster, street, or unit block — so work can progress without disrupting the entire community simultaneously.

A well-structured phasing plan should:

- Identify which buildings are in worst shape and prioritize accordingly
- Give individual homeowners advance notice (typically 48–72 hours) before work begins at their unit
- Account for Colorado's weather windows — our [exterior painting](/exterior-painting/) season in Northern Colorado runs roughly May through October, and scheduling needs to avoid early spring freeze risk and late-fall moisture
- Build in buffer days for unexpected weather or material delays

At Timnath Painting, we build phasing schedules into our HOA proposals upfront. Boards shouldn't be figuring out sequencing after the contract is signed — that's a contractor responsibility.

## Managing Community Expectations

This is where a lot of HOA paint projects quietly fall apart.

Homeowners don't always know a project is coming until a crew shows up outside their window. That creates friction — complaints to the board, cars in the way, pets not secured, residents upset about color choices they didn't get to weigh in on.

Clear communication protocols matter:

- **Pre-project notice** — Announce the project via HOA newsletter, email, or app at least 2–4 weeks out
- **Color approval process** — Final color selections should go through the architectural committee before any paint is purchased. Changes after work starts are expensive.
- **Day-of notification** — Residents near active work zones should get 48-hour notice to move vehicles and prepare
- **Progress updates** — A simple weekly update from the board keeps residents informed and complaints low

The painting contractor can help with logistics, but the communication structure lives with the board. Build it before the project starts.

## Colorado Climate Conditions: Why They Matter for HOA Projects

Northern Colorado's altitude means UV radiation runs **10–15% higher** than lower-elevation regions, and communities like Timnath experience **28+ freeze-thaw cycles per year**. That's hard on paint systems — especially on multi-unit buildings with north-facing walls that don't dry out as quickly.

For HOA projects, this means:

- Paint system selection matters — not all products hold up equally under Colorado conditions
- Surface prep (cleaning, caulking, priming) is non-negotiable, not optional
- Application timing should account for temperature minimums — most quality exterior paints require sustained temps above 50°F
