If you have a wood fence and you're wondering whether to stain it or paint it, you're asking exactly the right question — because the answer directly affects how long your fence lasts, how much maintenance you'll deal with, and what it'll cost you over the next decade.

For most homeowners in Fort Collins, Timnath, and Windsor, **staining is the smarter long-term move**. Here's why — backed by real-world performance data, not just opinion.

---

## How Each Option Works

Before you can compare them, you need to understand the fundamental difference.

**Paint** sits on top of the wood. It forms a film layer that seals the surface. That sounds protective — and it is, initially. But wood is a living material. It expands and contracts with temperature and moisture. Over time, that movement cracks the paint film. Once water gets underneath, you're dealing with peeling, blistering, and rot.

**Stain penetrates into the wood fibers.** Instead of forming a surface film, it saturates the grain. When the wood moves, the stain moves with it. There's no film to crack, no seal to break.

In Northern Colorado — where we see [28+ freeze-thaw cycles per year and UV radiation running 10–15% higher than lower-elevation regions](/fence-staining/) — that distinction isn't minor. It's the difference between a fence that lasts and one that needs attention every few years.

---

## Pros and Cons: Stain vs. Paint

### Fence Staining

**Pros:**
- Penetrates wood — won't peel, crack, or blister
- Breathable; allows moisture to escape without trapping it
- UV-resistant formulas protect against Colorado's intense sun
- Semi-transparent options enhance natural wood grain
- Easier recoating — no stripping or scraping required
- 3–5 year maintenance cycle for solid stains; semi-transparent may need touch-up sooner

**Cons:**
- Less color variety than paint (though solid stains cover this gap)
- Semi-transparent stains show weathering over time — intentional for some, not for others
- Won't hide existing damage or discoloration the way paint can

### Fence Painting

**Pros:**
- Maximum color control — any color imaginable
- Solid, clean look right out of the gate
- Can cover old stain (with proper prep) or hide significant discoloration

**Cons:**
- Film-forming — cracks and peels as wood expands and contracts
- Colorado's freeze-thaw cycles accelerate paint failure
- Repainting requires stripping peeling paint, which adds cost and labor every cycle
- Traps moisture if the surface seal is compromised, leading to rot
- Typically needs repainting every 3–5 years in this climate

## The Real Cost Comparison

Let's look at a typical 150–200 linear foot wood fence.

| | **Stain** | **Paint** |
|---|---|---|
| Initial application | $600–$1,200 | $800–$1,500 |
| Prep for recoat | Minimal — light cleaning | Heavy — scraping, sanding |
| Recoat cycle | Every 4–6 years | Every 3–5 years |
| 10-year cost estimate | $1,200–$2,000 | $2,000–$4,000+ |

The gap widens fast. Painting is more labor-intensive at every recoat, and the cycles are shorter in Colorado's climate. Over a decade, staining usually wins on cost — sometimes by a significant margin.

Most specify color — not finish type — so staining with a solid-color stain typically satisfies HOA guidelines while still outperforming paint on durability. ## What Type of Fence Does This Apply To?

This comparison is specifically about **natural wood fences** — cedar, pine, spruce. Composite, vinyl, and metal fencing have different rules entirely. For wood, penetrating stain is almost always the better long-term choice in this climate.

If your fence is already painted and you want to switch to stain, it's doable — but it requires proper prep to remove the existing paint film. That's something worth discussing before you commit to a direction.
