## Colorado Hail Is a Paint System Killer

If you've lived along the Front Range for more than a few summers, you already know the drill: dark skies roll in fast, and within minutes golf ball-sized hail is hammering your roof, your car, and your home's exterior. Most homeowners immediately think about roofing and windows. What often gets overlooked is what that hail does to your paint system — and why leaving it unaddressed is one of the more expensive mistakes you can make.

In Northern Colorado, [exterior painting](https://timnathpainting.com/exterior-painting/) isn't just cosmetic. A properly applied paint system is an active moisture barrier. Hail breaks that barrier. Spalling, cracking, and micro-fractures in the paint film — even when they look minor — let moisture in. Once that starts, you're dealing with wood rot, swelling, and substrate damage on top of the original hail impact. The repair bill grows fast.

---

## What Hail Actually Does to Exterior Paint

Standard contractor paint isn't engineered to take repeated impact. Here in Fort Collins and the surrounding region, we average more than 28 freeze-thaw cycles per year. Combine that stress with altitude-driven UV exposure (10–15% higher than at sea level) and a direct hailstrike, and you're looking at a compromised paint system — even if the surface looks okay from the street.

Signs to watch for after a storm:

- **Chipping or flaking** near siding seams and corners
- **Spatter marks** — small circular impact points where paint has been knocked loose
- **Exposed bare wood or substrate** anywhere on the surface
- **Soft or spongy siding** — indicates moisture intrusion has already started
- **Paint that feels chalky or rough** across large sections

Even if damage isn't immediately visible, hailstorms in the [Fort Collins area](https://timnathpainting.com/areas-served/fort-collins-co/) warrant a professional exterior inspection before assuming everything is fine.

## Documenting Damage for Your Insurance Claim

This is where most homeowners lose money they're entitled to. Insurance adjusters move fast. If you haven't documented the damage thoroughly before their visit, you may not recover the full cost of repair and repainting.

**Before the adjuster arrives:**

1. **Photograph everything the same day or within 24 hours.** Natural light, close-up shots, wide-angle establishing shots. Document every elevation of the house — front, back, both sides.
2. **Mark impact points.** Chalk circles around visible spatter marks or damaged areas make it easier to document density of impact.
3. **Note the date and reference the storm.** Pull local weather data (NOAA, Weather Underground) that confirms hail activity and size.
4. **Get a written estimate before your adjuster appointment** — not after. An independent estimate from a licensed contractor gives you leverage and documentation.

Most homeowners don't realize their paint system can be a covered loss under a standard HO-3 policy, especially when the damage is widespread and caused by a named weather event. Don't let the adjuster treat a full exterior repaint as a "cosmetic" issue if the paint system has been structurally compromised.

## What Has to Happen Before Repainting

Repainting over hail damage without addressing the substrate first is a waste of money and paint. Any competent contractor will tell you — and if they don't, walk away.

Before paint goes on, you need:

- **Surface repairs:** Replace or fill any damaged siding, boards, or trim. Paint won't bridge structural gaps.
- **Scraping and sanding:** All loose, cracked, or spalling paint has to come off completely.
- **Priming:** Any exposed wood or repaired sections must be primed before topcoat application.
- **Moisture testing:** If there's any softness in the substrate, test before sealing it behind a new paint system.

Skipping these steps means your new paint system fails early — and that failure won't be covered by your contractor's warranty or your insurance.

## Timing Matters in Colorado

One thing local homeowners often underestimate: timing the repaint around your adjuster visit and Colorado's weather windows.

Hailstorm season peaks in late spring through early summer. That's also optimal painting weather — temps above 50°F, low humidity, long dry windows. If you delay the repaint process past late September, you're pushing into freeze risk, which limits your product options and cure time.

Our recommendation: schedule your inspection and estimate immediately after a storm. Get your documentation together before the adjuster arrives. Once the claim is approved, move on repairs and repainting quickly — don't wait until fall and lose your window.
