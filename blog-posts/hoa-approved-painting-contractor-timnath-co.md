## Why Your HOA Gets a Say in Your Painting Project

If you live in a managed community in Timnath, Windsor, or anywhere else in Northern Colorado, your HOA isn't just a dues collector — it's a gatekeeper. Before a single drop of paint hits your siding, your association likely requires contractor approval. Skip that step and you could be looking at fines, forced repaints, or worse.

The good news: once you understand what HOAs are actually looking for, vetting contractors gets a lot simpler. Here's what matters.

---

## What HOAs Typically Require from Painting Contractors

Not every HOA operates the same way, but most managed communities in Colorado share a core set of contractor requirements. These fall into four buckets:

### 1. Licensing

Colorado doesn't issue a statewide painting contractor license, but HOAs still expect documentation. That typically means a valid business license, proof of registration with the state, and sometimes a local contractor registration depending on the city or county. If a painter can't produce paperwork on request, that's a red flag — and most HOA management companies will reject them outright.

### 2. Liability Insurance Minimums

This is where a lot of contractors fall short. HOAs commonly require a minimum of $1M in general liability coverage per occurrence — and some larger communities push that to $2M. This protects the association if property is damaged during the project.

Before you hire anyone, ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) and confirm the coverage limits match what your HOA requires. The COI should list your HOA or management company as an additional insured if that's in your community's guidelines.

Timnath Painting carries $2M in liability coverage — one of the higher thresholds you'll find with a local contractor, and a figure that clears the bar for virtually every HOA in Northern Colorado.

### 3. Experience With HOA Projects

HOA work has a different rhythm than a standard residential repaint. There are color approval processes, community-wide color palettes, noise ordinances, parking restrictions, and neighbor notification requirements. A contractor who's only worked on single-family jobs outside of HOA communities may not know how to navigate any of that.

When you're vetting a painter, ask directly: *Have you worked in HOA-managed communities before? Are you familiar with the approval process?* The answer tells you a lot. You can see examples of completed HOA and exterior work in our [project gallery](/gallery/).

### 4. Paint Product and Color Restrictions

Most HOAs maintain an approved color palette — often tied to a specific paint brand or product line. Your contractor needs to know how to work within those restrictions, not around them. Some communities go further and specify approved sheen levels, primer requirements, or the number of coats required.

This is especially relevant in Northern Colorado, where HOAs in newer communities like those around Timnath and Windsor often have detailed architectural guidelines. A contractor who pushes back on the approved palette or tries to substitute products without disclosure isn't someone you want involved.

---

## The Vetting Checklist Before You Submit for HOA Approval

When you've found a candidate, run through this before you submit anything to your HOA:

- **License documentation** — business license, state registration
- **COI with correct coverage limits** — confirm minimums match your HOA rules
- **Named additional insured** — if required by your HOA
- **Proof of prior HOA project experience** — ask for references or examples
- **Familiarity with your color palette** — confirm they'll source approved products
- **Written scope of work** — HOAs often require this as part of the approval packet

Getting this documentation upfront saves you from a back-and-forth with your management company and keeps your project timeline on track.
