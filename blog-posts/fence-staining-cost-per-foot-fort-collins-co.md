If you've started calling around for fence staining quotes in Fort Collins or Northern Colorado, you've probably noticed the numbers vary — sometimes by a lot. That's not a red flag on its own. Fence staining pricing has a lot of legitimate variables. This post breaks down what contractors are actually pricing when they give you a per-linear-foot number, what's typical for this region, and what to watch out for.

## What's the Average Fence Staining Cost Per Linear Foot?

For most residential wood fences in Northern Colorado, you can expect to pay **$2.50–$5.50 per linear foot** for professional staining. That range sounds wide, and it is — because it covers a lot of ground. Here's how it typically breaks down:

| Fence Type / Condition | Estimated Cost Per LF |
|---|---|
| 4-ft picket fence, good condition | $2.50–$3.50 |
| 6-ft privacy fence, good condition | $3.00–$4.50 |
| 6-ft privacy fence, heavy weathering | $4.00–$5.50 |
| 8-ft privacy fence, rough condition | $5.00–$6.50+ |
| Rail or split-rail fence | $2.00–$3.00 |

These are contractor rates for full-service work — prep, stain, and cleanup included. DIY material costs alone run $0.50–$1.00 per linear foot, but labor is where most of the cost lives.

## What Drives the Price Up or Down

### 1. Fence Height

This is the biggest factor. A 4-foot picket fence and a 6-foot privacy fence don't take the same amount of material or time — and an 8-foot fence can require two passes or ladder work, both of which add cost. Always confirm whether the quote is for one side or both.

### 2. Wood Species and Condition

Cedar and redwood stain cleanly and are common throughout Timnath, Windsor, and Fort Collins neighborhoods. Pine and pressure-treated lumber are more porous and may require an extra coat. Weathered or gray wood almost always needs cleaning or light sanding before stain will adhere properly — that prep work adds to the price but is not optional if you want the stain to last.

### 3. Prep Work Required

A quality contractor will clean the fence before staining — either by hand-washing, pressure washing, or chemical cleaning, depending on the wood's condition. In Colorado, UV exposure degrades the surface layer of wood quickly, and stain applied over a dirty or oxidized surface will peel within a season. If a quote seems unusually low, ask whether prep is included.

Our [fence staining process](/fence-staining/) includes surface prep as a standard step — not an add-on — because a properly prepped surface is what makes the 7–10 year system lifespan realistic.

### 4. Single-Sided vs. Double-Sided

Most residential fences share a property line. Staining one side is standard. Double-sided work can add 40–60% to the total cost, depending on fence style and access. Clarify this upfront.

### 5. Product Quality

There's a real difference between a $25/gallon big-box stain and a contractor-grade product formulated for high-UV, high-freeze-thaw environments. At elevation in Northern Colorado, the sun is more intense and winters put serious stress on wood. A stain that performs in Florida doesn't necessarily hold up the same way here. Ask your contractor what product they're using and why.

## How to Sanity-Check a Quote

Here's a simple way to evaluate any quote you receive:

1. **Get the total linear footage** — measure or ask the contractor to confirm it.
2. **Divide the total price by linear footage** — you should land in the $2.50–$5.50 range for a standard 6-ft fence. Outside that range, ask questions.
3. **Ask what's included** — cleaning, how many coats, product brand.
4. **Ask about return visits** — does the price include going back if the first coat doesn't cover evenly?

If a contractor can't answer those questions clearly, that's your signal.

## Fort Collins and Northern Colorado Specifics

Fences in this region take a beating. Timnath, Windsor, Severance, and the surrounding communities sit at around 4,900–5,000 feet. That elevation means UV radiation is roughly 10–15% higher than sea level, and the freeze-thaw cycle hits hard — often 28 or more times per season. A fence stained with the wrong product or without proper prep can start showing cracks, peeling, or gray-out within 2–3 years.

A properly done stain job using climate-appropriate products should last 5–7 years on a maintained fence. That's the benchmark to hold any contractor to. If they can't speak to why they've chosen their product for this climate, that's worth probing.
