## Why Most Commercial Painting Buyers Don't Know What They're Getting Into

If your business or property association has never hired a commercial exterior painter before, the process can feel like a black box. You get a bid, someone shows up with trucks and ladders, and a week later it either looks great or you're already seeing problems.

That's not good enough — especially when you're talking about a significant investment on a building that represents your brand.

This is a walkthrough of how a real commercial exterior painting job actually works, from the first site visit to the final walkthrough. If you're evaluating contractors for a [commercial painting project](/commercial-painting/) in Fort Collins CO or the surrounding area, this is what a professional process should look like.

---

## Step 1: Site Assessment

Every commercial job starts with a proper site assessment — not just a quick look and a number.

A thorough assessment covers:

- **Current surface condition** — Are there cracks, peeling, moisture damage, or failed caulking? These need to be documented before any coating conversation starts.
- **Substrate type** — Stucco, wood siding, metal panels, concrete block, and composite materials all have different prep requirements and coating compatibility.
- **Accessibility** — Does the job require lifts, scaffolding, or special equipment? This affects both timeline and cost.
- **Exposure profile** — In Northern Colorado, we're dealing with altitude UV intensity 10–15% higher than lower-elevation regions, plus significant freeze-thaw cycling. That affects coating selection.

This is also when a reputable contractor asks about your timeline, any HOA or municipality requirements, and whether phasing the work matters for your tenants or operations.

---

## Step 2: Coatings Specification

Once the site is assessed, the right contractor builds a coatings spec — not just picks a color.

"Spec" means: which primer, which topcoat, how many coats, and why. For commercial exteriors in the Fort Collins area, that usually means:

- A **penetrating primer** suited to the substrate
- A **high-build topcoat** with demonstrated UV resistance and flexibility for temperature cycling
- A **finish system rated for 7–10 years** under real Northern Colorado conditions — not just under controlled lab conditions

This is where experience with the local climate matters. A product that performs well in Houston won't necessarily hold up through a Colorado winter. When you review a bid, ask what system they're specifying and why.

---

## Step 3: Scheduling and Pre-Job Coordination

Commercial painting requires coordination that residential work doesn't. Before a crew ever shows up, a professional contractor should:

- Confirm paint delivery and material staging logistics
- Coordinate with you on crew access hours (especially if tenants are present)
- Pull any required permits
- Notify adjacent businesses or parking areas if work will affect them
- Set up proper signage and safety perimeter

Clear pre-job communication prevents the surprises that cause delays and tenant complaints.

---

## Step 4: Surface Preparation

This is where most of the work actually happens — and where corners get cut by less serious contractors.

Proper surface prep on a commercial exterior typically includes:

- **Pressure washing** to remove dirt, chalking, mildew, and loose coating
- **Scraping and sanding** any failing paint back to a stable surface
- **Caulking and sealing** gaps, joints, and penetrations
- **Spot priming** bare or repaired areas before full-coat primer
- **Masking** windows, doors, signage, and any surfaces not being painted

Skipping or rushing this step is the #1 reason paint fails early. A 7–10 year system only delivers if it's applied over properly prepared surfaces.

---

## Step 5: Application

Application sequencing matters. A professional crew follows:

1. **Full prime coat** on all prepared surfaces
2. **First topcoat** applied at spec thickness (check the product data sheet — mil thickness matters)
3. **Second topcoat** (required by most quality systems)
4. **Detail and trim work** completed after field coats

Weather windows matter here too. In Northern Colorado, paint shouldn't be applied below 50°F or above 90°F. Scheduling around temperature and humidity — not just around crew availability — is part of doing the job right.

---

## Step 6: Quality Control Inspection

A professional contractor doesn't just pack up when the last brush stroke is done. Final QC should include:

- **Visual inspection** of all surfaces for holidays (missed spots), drips, or inadequate coverage
- **Touch-up pass** to correct any deficiencies
- **Walkthrough with the property owner or manager** — you should be looking at the finished product together, not receiving a phone call saying it's done
- **Documentation** of the system applied, colors used, and warranty terms

If a contractor isn't doing a formal closeout walkthrough, that's a yellow flag.
